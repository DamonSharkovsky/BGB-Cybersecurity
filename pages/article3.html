What Happened:

This morning at 9:30 AM, I received a WhatsApp video call from what appeared to be my long-time business partner, David. The video quality was excellent, the lighting looked natural, and it was definitely his face and voice. He appeared to be in his home office, which I recognized.

"Hey mate, I'm in a bit of a bind. Our client payment went through but there's an issue with the bank transfer system. I need you to wire R75,000 to our backup account immediately. I'll send you the details. We need to resolve this before the client notices any delays."

Why It Almost Worked:

  • Perfect visual match: The person looked exactly like David - facial features, expressions, even his mannerisms
  • Familiar environment: The background showed his home office that I've seen in countless video calls
  • Voice cloning: The voice was spot-on, including his South African accent and speech patterns
  • Business context: The scenario was plausible - we handle large client payments regularly
  • Time pressure: Created urgency by mentioning client expectations

The Red Flags I Nearly Missed:

  1. Slight delay in responses: There was a 1-2 second delay when I asked questions, like processing time
  2. Generic references: He called me "mate" instead of my usual nickname
  3. Avoided specifics: When I asked about the client name, he gave vague responses
  4. Video quality inconsistency: His face looked slightly too smooth compared to the background
  5. No interruptions: Real David would have interrupted or talked over me at some point

What Saved Me:

I asked him about our conversation from yesterday regarding his daughter's school play. The "David" on the call gave a generic response about how it went well, but the real David's daughter is actually in university, not school. That's when I realized this was fake.

How They Did It (My Investigation):

  • Scraped photos and videos from David's LinkedIn and company website
  • Used publicly available videos of him speaking at industry conferences
  • Created a convincing digital replica using deepfake technology
  • Researched our business relationship through social media and public records
  • Spoofed his WhatsApp number (showed his profile picture and name)

Protection Steps for Business Owners:

  1. Establish verification protocols: Set up secret phrases or questions for financial requests
  2. Use multiple communication channels: Always verify through phone, email, and in-person when possible
  3. Question urgency: Scammers love time pressure - slow down and verify
  4. Test with personal details: Ask questions only the real person would know
  5. Look for technical tells: Slight delays, overly smooth skin, unnatural eye movements
  6. Implement financial safeguards: Require dual approval for transfers over certain amounts
  7. Regular team training: Keep staff updated on emerging scam techniques

What I'm Doing Now:

  • Implementing a company-wide verification protocol for all financial requests
  • Setting up dual authorization requirements for wire transfers over R10,000
  • Training all employees on deepfake recognition
  • Limiting the amount of business-related video content we post publicly
  • Reporting this to the police and our bank's fraud department

Please share this with other business owners! This technology is becoming more accessible and scammers are specifically targeting businesses because of the potential for larger payouts. Trust your instincts - if something feels off, it probably is.

Update: I've contacted the real David and we're now implementing security measures together. He was horrified that his likeness was used this way.
